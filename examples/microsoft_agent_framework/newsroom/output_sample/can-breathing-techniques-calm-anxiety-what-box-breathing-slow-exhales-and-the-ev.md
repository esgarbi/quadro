# Can Breathing Techniques Calm Anxiety? What Box Breathing, Slow Exhales, and the Evidence Really Show

Anxiety is not just a thought pattern. It is also a body state: a racing heart, tight chest, shallow breaths, dizziness, restlessness. That is one reason breathing techniques have become so popular. They seem to offer something immediate and concrete when the mind feels hard to control.

But do they actually work? The short answer is: sometimes, and in specific ways. Breathing practices such as box breathing and slow, extended exhalation can reduce the physical intensity of anxiety for many people, especially in the moment. The science is promising, though not uniform, and breathwork is best understood as a tool rather than a cure-all.

## Why Breathing Changes How Anxiety Feels in the Body

Breathing is unusual because it sits at the border of automatic and voluntary control. Most of the time, the body handles it without conscious effort. But we can also deliberately change its speed and depth, and that can affect the autonomic nervous system — the system that helps regulate arousal, heart rate, and the body’s stress response.

When anxiety rises, breathing often becomes quick, shallow, and chest-focused. This pattern can amplify sensations people already fear: lightheadedness, tingling, chest discomfort, and a sense of not getting enough air. Slowing breathing, especially in a way that encourages diaphragmatic breathing — the lower, fuller breathing that expands the abdomen — may help interrupt that loop.

Clinical research supports this body-level effect. In a randomized trial involving patients with anxiety disorder, slow breathing exercises improved markers of cardiac autonomic regulation, suggesting a shift toward a calmer physiological state [Kavitha et al., 2024]. A pilot randomized controlled trial on deep diaphragmatic breathing also found neurobiological and anti-inflammatory effects, adding to the idea that breathing may influence stress systems beyond simple relaxation [Maniaci et al., 2024].

This does not mean breathing “turns off” anxiety. It means it may change the body terrain in which anxiety unfolds.

## Box Breathing vs. Slow Exhales: What These Techniques Actually Do

Not all breathing techniques are trying to do the same thing.

**Box breathing** usually follows a four-part pattern: inhale, hold, exhale, hold, each for the same count, often four seconds. It is structured and easy to remember. For some people, that structure is part of the appeal. It gives the mind a task and can feel grounding during stress.

**Slow-exhale breathing** usually emphasizes making the exhale longer than the inhale — for example, inhaling for four seconds and exhaling for six or eight. This approach is based on the idea that slower breathing, and especially prolonged exhalation, may support parasympathetic activity, the “rest-and-regulate” side of the autonomic system.

A broader psychophysiology literature has long suggested that paced breathing can affect heart rate variability, or HRV, a measure linked to autonomic flexibility. More recent work reinforces that point. In a five-week HRV biofeedback intervention, changes in negative emotions were mediated by changes in resting HRV, suggesting that physiologic regulation and emotional improvement may move together [Jung et al., 2025]. Lehrer argues that these psychophysiological methods deserve a larger role in psychotherapy precisely because they can give people a measurable way to influence stress-related body responses [Lehrer, 2025].

So which is better: box breathing or slow exhales? There is no clear winner from the current evidence. Box breathing may help by imposing rhythm and attentional focus. Slow exhales may be especially useful when someone feels physically revved up and wants to reduce arousal. The better technique is often the one a person can do comfortably and repeat consistently.

## What Clinical Studies Show About Paced Breathing and Anxiety

The evidence base for breathing and anxiety is encouraging, but it is not as definitive as social media often implies.

Some clinical studies show real benefits. A 2024 study found guided breathing exercises were effective in reducing anxiety, stress, and depression in patients with COVID-19 [Sheikh et al., 2024]. In anxiety disorder specifically, slow breathing improved autonomic function in a randomized controlled trial [Kavitha et al., 2024]. These findings fit with the idea that paced breathing can help downshift stress reactivity.

But important limits remain. Studies often use different breathing rates, session lengths, instructions, and populations, making comparisons hard. Some focus on physiological outcomes, others on self-reported anxiety. Sample sizes are sometimes small, and not every study tests whether breathing outperforms a good comparison condition like quiet rest.

That matters. In adolescents recovering from acute stress, one study found differences between app-guided breathing and natural relaxation, raising a practical question: how much of the benefit comes from the breathing pattern itself, and how much from simply pausing, resting, and being guided through recovery [Schleicher et al., 2024]? Another trial found audiovisual stimulation could improve mood and cognition as an accessible alternative to breath-focused meditation, suggesting breath is not the only route to regulation [Johnson et al., 2024].

In other words, breathing techniques likely help many people, but they are not uniquely magical. They are one accessible regulation tool among several.

## When Breathwork Helps, When It Backfires, and Who Should Be Cautious

Breathwork tends to help most when anxiety shows up as a body surge: rapid breathing, muscle tension, racing heart, or a mounting sense of panic. In those moments, a simple paced-breath pattern can provide a bridge between “I’m overwhelmed” and “I can get through the next minute.”

It may be especially useful:
- during acute stress
- before a difficult conversation or presentation
- at bedtime, when physical tension keeps the mind activated
- as part of therapy, especially when paired with skills for noticing and reframing anxious thoughts

But breathwork can also backfire. Some people become more anxious when they pay close attention to breathing, especially if they are prone to panic attacks, health anxiety, or sensations of air hunger. Breath holds, including the holds built into box breathing, may feel uncomfortable or threatening for those people. If a technique makes you feel trapped, dizzy, or more alarmed, that is not a sign you are failing; it may simply be the wrong tool.

There are also reasons to be cautious. People with respiratory or cardiac conditions should check with a clinician before doing more intensive breathing practices. And anyone with a history of trauma may find inward-focused exercises unexpectedly activating. In those cases, external anchors — looking around the room, naming objects, holding something cold — may be better starting points than tracking the breath.

That caution is consistent with the broader message from the evidence: breathing can support regulation, but it is not a replacement for appropriate care. If anxiety is persistent, disabling, or accompanied by depression, insomnia, substance use, or thoughts of self-harm, professional help matters.

## A Simple, Evidence-Aligned Way to Test Breathing Techniques for Yourself

If you want to try breathing for anxiety, keep the experiment simple.

Start with **one technique for one week**, once or twice a day, plus any time anxiety spikes. Two reasonable options:

**Option 1: Slow exhales**
- Inhale through the nose for 4 seconds
- Exhale slowly for 6 seconds
- Continue for 3 to 5 minutes

**Option 2: Gentle box breathing**
- Inhale for 4 seconds
- Hold for 2 to 4 seconds
- Exhale for 4 seconds
- Hold for 2 to 4 seconds
- Continue for 1 to 3 minutes

A few evidence-aligned rules:
- **Stay comfortable.** Do not force very deep breaths; overbreathing can make symptoms worse.
- **Short is fine.** A few minutes may be enough to test whether your body responds.
- **Track what happens.** Before and after, rate anxiety from 0 to 10. Also note physical signs like heart racing, chest tightness, or dizziness.
- **Choose fit over hype.** If box breathing feels too effortful, try longer exhales instead. If focusing on the breath is irritating, consider other calming methods.

The goal is not to achieve perfect calm. It is to see whether a breathing pattern reliably lowers the intensity of your stress response by even one or two notches. That is often enough to make the next choice — speaking, resting, calling someone, using a therapy skill — more manageable.

Breathing techniques deserve neither dismissal nor overstatement. The evidence suggests they can meaningfully reduce the bodily intensity of anxiety for many people, likely by influencing autonomic regulation and related stress systems [Kavitha et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2025]. But the science is still evolving, and results vary. Think of breathwork as a practical first-aid skill: low-cost, portable, often helpful, and best used as part of a broader approach to mental health.
