# Can Cyclic Sighing Calm Anxiety in 5 Minutes? What the Research Says About This Fast Breathing Reset

When anxiety spikes, people are often told to “just take a deep breath.” The advice is common, but the science is more specific: certain breathing patterns seem to shift the body out of a high-alert state surprisingly quickly. One of the most discussed lately is **cyclic sighing**, a simple practice built around repeated long exhalations. It is not a cure for anxiety disorders, and it is not magic. But emerging research suggests it may be a useful, fast-acting reset for stress and anxious arousal.

## Why Fast Anxiety Relief Is Physiological, Not Just Psychological

Anxiety is not only a story in the mind. It is also a body state.

When stress rises, the autonomic nervous system tilts toward sympathetic activation—the “fight, flight, or freeze” response. Heart rate may climb, breathing can become shallow or rapid, muscles tighten, and attention narrows toward threat. That is one reason anxiety can feel so immediate and physical. It is also why physical interventions, including breathwork, may help interrupt it.

Research on breathing-based interventions increasingly points to measurable changes in physiological arousal, not just subjective calm. In a 2023 study, brief daily structured breathing practices improved mood and reduced respiratory rate more effectively than mindfulness meditation alone over the study period [Balban et al., 2023]. In people with anxiety disorder, slow breathing exercises have also been associated with improved cardiac autonomic function, suggesting a shift toward healthier parasympathetic regulation—the branch linked to rest and recovery [Kavitha et al., 2024].

That matters because if anxiety shows up in the body first, relief may need to begin there too. Breathwork does not erase the causes of stress—relationship strain, financial pressure, trauma, illness—but it may lower the body’s alarm volume enough to help people think more clearly.

## What Exactly Is Cyclic Sighing?

Cyclic sighing is a controlled breathing technique that repeats a particular pattern:

1. Inhale through the nose  
2. Take a second small inhale to fully expand the lungs  
3. Exhale slowly and fully, usually longer than the inhale  
4. Repeat for several minutes

If that sounds familiar, it resembles the body’s natural sigh reflex. Humans spontaneously sigh throughout the day, and these sighs appear to help reinflate tiny air sacs in the lungs and regulate breathing rhythm. Cyclic sighing turns that natural pattern into a deliberate exercise.

The best-known recent human study on the technique comes from Stanford researchers, who compared cyclic sighing with other brief breathing practices and mindfulness meditation. Over the intervention period, cyclic sighing produced the greatest improvement in daily positive mood and the largest reduction in respiratory rate among the groups studied [Balban et al., 2023].

Importantly, cyclic sighing is not simply “taking deep breaths.” The emphasis is on the **extended exhale**. That seems to be part of what makes it calming: prolonged exhalation is associated with increased parasympathetic activity, which may help slow the heart and reduce the body’s threat response [Kavitha et al., 2024].

## How Breathing Patterns Influence Stress, CO2, and Heart Rate Variability

To understand why cyclic sighing may work within minutes, it helps to know three basics: stress physiology, carbon dioxide, and heart rate variability.

First, breathing is one of the few automatic body functions we can consciously control. That gives it unusual leverage over the nervous system. Slow, deliberate breathing—especially with longer exhalations—can reduce physiological arousal and influence heart rhythm patterns tied to emotional regulation [Balban et al., 2023; Kavitha et al., 2024].

Second, **carbon dioxide (CO2)** matters. Many anxious people unknowingly overbreathe or hyperventilate, which can lower CO2 too much. That drop may contribute to lightheadedness, tingling, chest discomfort, and a sense of panic. While cyclic sighing involves deep breaths, it is not meant to be fast or forceful. The goal is not to “blow off” as much air as possible, but to settle into slower, more efficient ventilation. In practical terms, gentleness matters. Overdoing any breathing exercise can backfire by making symptoms worse.

Third, there is **heart rate variability**, or HRV—the small variation in time between heartbeats. Higher HRV is generally considered a sign of more flexible autonomic regulation. Slow breathing practices have been shown to improve cardiac autonomic measures in people with anxiety disorders, suggesting a more resilient stress response [Kavitha et al., 2024]. Deep diaphragmatic breathing has also been linked in pilot research to neurobiological and anti-inflammatory effects consistent with reduced stress load [Maniaci et al., 2024].

None of this means one breathing pattern is a universal fix. But it does support the idea that a five-minute breathing practice can produce real physiological shifts, not just the feeling of “doing something healthy.”

## What the Best Human Studies Show So Far

The evidence for cyclic sighing is promising, but still early.

The strongest direct evidence comes from Balban and colleagues’ 2023 randomized study of brief structured respiration practices. Participants practiced for about five minutes daily over several weeks. Compared with mindfulness meditation, breathwork groups showed greater improvements in mood and decreases in respiratory rate, with **cyclic sighing standing out as particularly effective** among the breathing techniques tested [Balban et al., 2023].

That does not prove cyclic sighing will stop an acute anxiety attack in exactly five minutes for everyone. But it suggests the method can meaningfully reduce physiological arousal and improve emotional state when practiced regularly, with effects that may be noticeable quickly.

A 2025 pilot randomized controlled trial adds another clue from a different setting: cyclic sighing in a clinic waiting room appeared to reduce pain perception, supporting the idea that this breathing style may rapidly alter stress-related bodily experience [Hanley et al., 2025]. Pain and anxiety are not the same, but they share overlapping stress pathways.

Broader breathing research also supports the mechanism. Slow breathing exercises in people with anxiety disorder improved markers of cardiac autonomic function in a randomized trial [Kavitha et al., 2024]. And other mind-body breathing traditions, including diaphragmatic breathing and related practices, have shown effects on stress biology such as cortisol and inflammatory signaling, though these studies often examine broader programs rather than cyclic sighing alone [Maniaci et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024].

So what is the bottom line? Current studies suggest cyclic sighing is a credible short-form calming tool. But the evidence base is still small, and we need more trials in people with diagnosed anxiety disorders, panic symptoms, and real-world high-stress situations.

## How to Try a 5-Minute Cyclic Sighing Practice Safely

If you want to test it, keep it simple.

**Try this for five minutes:**

- Sit upright or lie down somewhere comfortable  
- Relax your shoulders and jaw  
- Inhale gently through your nose  
- Add a second small sniff-like inhale to top off the breath  
- Exhale slowly through the mouth or nose until the lungs feel comfortably empty  
- Pause briefly, then repeat

A few tips make it safer and more effective:

- **Keep the breath soft, not dramatic.** Forcing giant breaths can make you dizzy.
- **Let the exhale be longer than the inhale.** That is the calming part for many people.
- **Stop if you feel worse.** Breathwork should not intensify panic, chest pain, or faintness.
- **Practice when relatively calm first.** It is easier to learn before you need it during a stressful moment.
- **Use it as a reset, not a test.** The goal is not perfect technique. It is to lower activation.

For some people, one to five minutes may be enough to feel a shift in heart rate, muscle tension, or mental agitation. For others, the benefit may be subtler: a little more space before reacting.

## When Breathwork Is Not Enough: Red Flags and Next Steps

Breathwork is a tool, not treatment for every kind of anxiety.

If anxiety is frequent, worsening, or disrupting sleep, work, school, eating, or relationships, it deserves broader care. The same is true if you are having panic attacks, avoiding normal activities, using alcohol or drugs to cope, or feeling depressed or hopeless.

Seek prompt medical care if symptoms include:

- chest pain
- fainting
- severe shortness of breath
- new heart palpitations
- thoughts of self-harm

Breathing exercises can also feel uncomfortable for some people with trauma histories, asthma, chronic lung disease, or panic disorder. If that is you, consider learning with a clinician, therapist, or trauma-informed instructor rather than pushing through alone.

The encouraging news is that breathwork can fit into a bigger plan. Used alongside therapy, exercise, medication when needed, better sleep, and reduced caffeine or alcohol, cyclic sighing may be a practical, low-cost way to interrupt stress in the moment.

A five-minute breathing reset will not solve the source of anxiety. But when used wisely, it may help quiet the body enough for the next helpful step.
